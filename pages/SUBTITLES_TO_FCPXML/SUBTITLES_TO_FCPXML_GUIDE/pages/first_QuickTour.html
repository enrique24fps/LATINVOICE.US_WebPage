Step by Step Guide

First: A Quick Look into the Graphic Interface

There are four main areas numbered from 1 to 4 and two big buttons to the right: Generate XML and Exit,
those buttons do exactly what those words mean, but before you generate your XML and quit the program,
you must load a subtitle file (in step 4) and make sure that all settings from 1 to 4 are as desired.

Step 1 - Settings for FCP X

Final Cut Pro uses Libraries, Events and Projects as a structure to organize any job.
A Library is a container for Events and in turn Events contain Projects and videos.
A Project has all the editing decisions (like in and out times for a subtitle over a video)
needed to playback or export your video exactly as you want.
In order to do a subtitle layback we need to import a subtitle layback Project as an Event with a Project
containing an XML file with the subtitles already generated as Basic Title assets and all the editing decisions already made.

For that purpose we should name the Event and the Project we will generate.
My Subtitles and My Subtitle Project are the default names provided by the software.
You can customize these names to identify them in FCP X.

Be sure to know your video’s resolution and frame rate values when setting up Format and resolution and Frame rate.
Also, remember that the subtitle’s timing must match the video’s frame rate.

In order to open your video on QuickTime, right-click your video’s name on the Finder and then choose
Open with > QuickTime Player.

Then, on QuickTime, open the inspector by pressing cmd + i
or by going to the menu bar and select Window > Show Movie Inspector.

On the inspector Format section you will see the resolution values next to the codec’s name.
On the image below you can see that resolution values are 1920 x 1080, which correspond to an HD video.

The frame rate is labeled as FPS, which stands for Frames Per Second. On the image above it’s 23.98.

If the resolution values don’t match the software’s exactly, you can apply the closest available.
This way, an image scale will be automatically generated on FCP X when dragging the video.
But remember that frame rate values must match.

Note: it is not recommended but you will notice that frames rates 23.98, 24, 29.97 and 30
sometimes they can fit each other by adjusting times or not in Step 3, but it is not a clean job
because you are missing some frames.

Step 2 - Font Properties

This step is pretty straightforward. Default values are a very good option for your HD videos.
Choose the font you prefer from the drop down menu: Arial, Helvetica Neue, Gill Sans, Lucida Sans
or Avenir Next are all good options.
Choose the font color (we recommend white or yellow, as these provide a nice contrast).
Then choose a font size.
Line space refers to the actual separation between two lines of text in a subtitle.
Increase or decrease the line space as you see fit to improve reader’s comfort.
Choose an outline color. Black does a very good job keeping the subtitle over the video,
and thus, makes it easier to read.
Finally, adjust the size of the outline to increase separation against the video.

Step 3 - Adjust and Select Format

This step has four options. Default values work very well for most cases. This is a quite technical section.

Setting 1- Subtitles times adjustment.
There are two ways of measuring time of NTSC videos: drop frame and non drop frame.
Although the video remains the same, the inner clock associated to that particular video
runs slower or faster depending on the option you choose. Sometimes your subtitles' timing
comes from a drop frame video source, but your actual video runs over the other clock.
When this happens, your subtitles will progressively display earlier or later than they should
at a rate of approximately two frames per minute (around 20 frames every 10 minutes or 3.5 seconds per hour).
The exact factor in this cases is 1000/1001 or 1001/1000 the other way around.
When this happens, just choose the proper option from the drop-down menu to compensate the difference.

Setting 2-Select a subtitle format
This version of SUBTITLES TO FCPXML supports STL, EBU, TXT and SRT subtitle formats.
If your subtitles are encoded in any other format, you should try our subtitle converter software.

It is important to remember that this version of the software only accepts one or two lines per subtitle.
Three or more lines are not accepted.

STL EBU format is a platform-free format. It has its own character tables and supports the following alphabets:
Latin, Latin/Cyrillic, Latin/Arabic, Latin/Greek and Latin/Hebrew.
This version of the software supports only the Latin alphabet. If you have any other alphabet,
you must convert your STL EBU to TXT (UTF 8) in order to do the subtitle layback.

When selecting the TXT or SRT formats, pay special attention to the text encoding labeled (WIN), (MAC) or (UTB).
Failure in choosing the right text encoding will lead to misplaced letters and accent marks, or too many line errors.
If this is the case, try again with the proper encoding.

We strongly suggest the use of UTF8 text encoding for your subtitling since it’s a platform-free format
and offers a larger amount of characters than the alternatives, such as the musical note (♪),
which is very often used in Closed Captioning.

See the Subtitles and Subtitle Formats section in this guide for further information on industry standards
for commands and formats, as well as good practices for subtitles preparation.
A link is provided at the bottom of this page.

Setting 3-Italics text format
There are two options in the drop-down menu: “[ and ]” and “( and )”.
Choose the one that corresponds to your subtitle preparation.
The “[ and ]” option means that when the character “[” appears, italics style should begin.
When “]” appears, italics style should end and regular style should be used from that point on.

The default font style is regular, so when italics style is used, it will stop at the end of the line
whether it is marked by a “]” or not. Thus, the next line will always start with regular style.

The same applies for “( and )”, only that “(” and “)” characters are used instead.

These two options are used because sometimes the above-mentioned characters are used for different purposes,
such as enclosing sound effects or speaker ID’s: (huge laugh) or (Peter) Hi, what’s up?, for instance.
Or viceversa: [huge laugh] or [Peter] Hi, what’s up?.
This is why you should label italics accordingly to avoid any confusion.

Setting 4 -STL raised are in line or less
This option is used when you have an STL EBU file as a subfile source.

Vertical position in the STL EBU format is defined in two ways: as in -vision with values ranging from 0-99,
or as teletext with values ranging from 1-12. This number represents the number of rows from the top of the screen
to the bottom subtitle row. SUBTITLE TO FCPX uses the most common, teletext, and reads its vertical positions
and translates them to the two vertical positions that the software supports for layback: TOP and BOTTOM.

Since STL EBU has 12 vertical positions, “1” stands for the top of the screen, “6” for the middle,
and “11” for the bottom (because we have two text lines i.e. 11 and 12).
Thus, when you choose 6 in the drop down menu, it means that any subtitle with a vertical position equal or less than 6
will be at the TOP position in your layback, and any vertical position bigger than 6 will be at the bottom of the screen.

Step 4 - Select File

In order to generate an XML file, first you need to load a subtitle file.

Use the button Load a file to browse and select a subtitle file in the format selected in Step 3.
Once you’ve loaded a subfile, its name will appear under the Selected file: label.
If you haven’t loaded a file, the line There Is No File Selected will read under this label.

Once the file is loaded, press the button Generate XML.

If you change the subfile after loading it or make any changes in the settings 1 through 4,
you must reload the file by pressing the Reload button, or you can simply load the file again.

You can also clear the loaded file by pressing Clear selection.

In the subtitling industry, it’s common practice to mark the so-called forced subtitles
with the character “{” at the beginning of the first line of a subtitle.
Forced subtitles are usually UPPERCASE-only subtitles that translate signs (billboards, road signs),
text (the title of the movie), and/or burned-ins from a previous subtitling
(for example, when there’s a scene in a different language from the rest of the movie).

If you want to layback only the forced subtitles in a subfile, simply mark the Only forceds box.

Step 5 - Generate XML

Once your settings are as desired and you have loaded a subfile,
press the button Generate XML and a window will appear where you can write the name of your file.
Then, proceed to select the location where you want to save the .fcpxml file
that you will import into Final Cut Pro X.

What To Do Next

Open Final Cut Pro X, set up your layback and, using Compressor, export a video with the subtitles burned into it.

Below, you’ll find a couple of chapters that will walk you through the subtitle layback in FCPX
and provide you with an example on how to export a video using Compressor.
